The Kasiki-Babbage Test

After nearly three centuries of total security, Charles Babbage and Freidrich Kasiki cracked the cipher independently during the mid-1800's. The attack hinges upon being able to find repeated strings within the characters. For instance, take the following ciphertext

FGXWENFJFRDSGFGXJFGXWEJLJKFGXJJKWEJNKJG

The savvy codebreaker will notice that the sequence FGX, which might correspond to a common three-letter sequence in English such as "the", is repeated every thirteenth letter. Such a repetition strongly indicates that a thirteen-letter key is being used. After this, all that remains is to break the original ciphertext into thirteen subgroups, with the first subgroup consisting of the first letter of each thirteen-letter block (WJJ), the second consisting of the second letters (EFJ), and so on.

These subgroups of letters have effectively been enciphered with monosubstitution ciphers, and thus by applying frequency analysis we can decipher the subgroups and therefore the Vigenere Cipher
